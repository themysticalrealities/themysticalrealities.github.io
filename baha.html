The Baha'i Faith

Baha'is believe in the unity of all races, the elimination of prejudice, the equality of women and men, universal education, the elimination of extremes of wealth and poverty, a spiritual solution to economic problems, the harmony of science and religion, the independent investigation of truth, and that all people must work together for the common benefit of humanity.

A Brief History

The Bahá'í faith is one of the youngest of the world's major religions. It was founded by Bahá'u'lláh in Iran in 1863. Iran was then mainly a Muslim country, and the faith was proclaimed by a young Persian by the name of Ali Muhammad, who called himself The Báb (Meaning "The Gate" in Arabic). He declared that a messenger would soon arrive from God, who would be the latest in a line of prophets including Moses, Jesus Christ, and Muhammad.

Bahá'u'lláh, which means the Glory of God in Arabic, was born Mirza Husayn Ali in 1817. Bahá'ís believe that Bahá'u'lláh is the Messenger of God for today. However Bahá'u'lláh himself stated that he is not God's final messenger.

At the center of the Baha'i faith is Unity of all humanity. Bahá'u'lláh taught that God intervenes throughout human history at different times to reveal more of himself through his messengers (called Divine Messengers, or Manifestations of God). The central idea of the faith is that of unity. They believe that people should work together for the common benefit of humanity.
